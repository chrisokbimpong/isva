Sarbanes-Oxley contains 11 titles that describe specific mandates and requirements for financial reporting. Which title enforces IT security controls and explain how these controls can be implemented to protect banking assets.

1. Section 404 of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act mandates IT security procedures. According to Section 404, all publicly listed firms must create internal financial reporting controls and processes, as well as record, test, and maintain those controls and procedures to verify their effectiveness. To comply with section 404, the SOX404 top-down risk assessment (TDRA) is conducted. It entails evaluating internal controls using particular risk variables to determine the extent and evidence needed. The following are some examples of controls that are commonly included in the scope:

 

• A specific application control approach that immediately mitigates financial reporting hazards detected. Such restrictions assist prohibit programs that execute certain financial activities, such as accounts payable, payroll, and general ledger, from interacting with the banking sector and other financial institutions.

• IT general controls that back up the claim that the software works as expected and that important financial data are accurate. This increases banking and financial institutions' accountability, authenticity, integrity, and availability, while also protecting assets by restricting the likelihood of information manipulation or fabrication by insiders within the bank.

 

• IT operations controls, which guarantee that processing issues are detected and resolved. These measures protect bank assets by reducing the likelihood of data disruption.

2. Obtain the support and cooperation of high management. This is critical because it guarantees that risk assessments are taken seriously at the lowest levels of the business. It also guarantees that resources are available to conduct the program and that the findings of the evaluation result in the adoption of suitable policy and control adjustments. At the commencement of a new assessment, top management will give support in establishing the scope of the assessment and the participants. At the end, approving the activities established in response to the recommendations.

 

Make a focal point. appointing a focal point to monitor and manage the organization's risk assessment process (all focal points are either groups or individuals) positioned at the corporate level or are members of a corporate level committee that coordinates the risk assessment's development from a company-wide perspective). The focus point will make the organization's risk assessment program's planning, performance, and reporting easier.

Procedures should be defined.

Each business must design protocols for performing risk assessments that are specified and recorded, as well as instruments to help facilitate and stabilize the process. This, in conjunction with the focal point, will aid in the oversight of the process, ensuring assessment uniformity and preventing separate business units from changing the already established method each time a new assessment is necessary.

Experts from the business and technical worlds should be consulted. Because business managers have the best understanding of the criticality and sensitivity of individual business operations, as well as the systems and data that support those operations, involving business and technical experts is critical. They are also in the best position to assess the impact of system misuse or disruption. Technical experts, such as security specialists, who are brought into the process have a thorough grasp of the present system's architecture and weaknesses, as well as the possible advantages, costs, and performance impacts of additional controls under consideration. As a result, meetings involving these two and a variety of other individuals in business operations and processes, security, information resource management, information technology, and systems operations during the risk assessment process produce excellent results that, among other things, define the scope of the risk assessment process.

Hold the business unit accountable. The business unit is in charge of initiating and performing risk assessments, as well as following up on the suggestions that are made as a consequence. They are seen to be in the best position to identify when an assessment is required and to ensure that the assessment's recommendations for risk reduction approaches are properly implemented.

Individual evaluation scope is being limited. In most cases, businesses will do a series of focused evaluations on specific company areas. This is done to make risk assessment scheduling and execution more manageable. When compared to doing a single major risk assessment that covers all of an organization's operations at the same time. It also allows companies to rank units in order to decide the order in which risk assessments should be conducted and who should be assessed more frequently.

Keep a record of the outcome. The results of risk assessments are documented and kept on file so that managers may be held accountable for their actions and a permanent record can be kept. Risk assessment records are kept on file to serve as a starting point for future risk assessments and as a quick source of information for new managers in the business unit. Others, such as the internal audit department, might use the documentation to confirm that organizational units are following the company's policies.

As the next layer of protection, they supplement preventative and access restrictions such as firewalls. System and information controls, audit and accountability controls, and incident controls are all implemented. The adoption of these controls allows security professionals to respond quickly to suspected insider alterations to the organization's data. This helps to maintain the system's integrity.

Firewalls are reference monitors that are used to keep track of all traffic. They filter all communication between an internal network (which is normally secured) and an external network such as the internet (usually the internet). By blocking or allowing data flow between two sections of a network architecture, they create access controls and ensure the confidentiality and availability of a system and the information it contains.

On a shared network, VPNs (virtual private networks) replicate the security of a dedicated, secured communication connection. They enforce and put in place system and communication security rules. They protect the security and availability of data going across a shared network by using link encryption to prevent illegal access.

4b. Integrity controls: by adopting rules or processes to safeguard health information from inappropriate or illegal change or deletion, these controls assist limit the risk of data modification. Identifying all users who have been granted access to the system, as well as any potential unauthorized sources who may be able to intercept and change data.

Implementing controls or rules for electronic information systems that store health information to enable access to individuals or programs who have been granted access rights and prevent access to those who do not mitigates the risk of disclosure of personal information and data availability.

Person or entity authentication measures: reduces the risk of personal information being disclosed by putting in place controls or procedures to authenticate the identification of those attempting to access the health information system.

Facility Access Controls: mitigates the risk of personal information disclosure and data availability by implementing controls or procedures that restrict physical access to health information systems and the facilities in which they are housed, and ensure that only properly authorized individuals have access.

Audit controls: install hardware, software, procedures, or protocols that record and review real-time activity on health information systems to reduce the risk of data manipulation.

Security controls

·      Access controls. These prevent unauthorized, individuals from gaining access to sensitive or crucial information or systems that contain such information. The implementation of access controls protects the disclosure of information and availability of data, thus promoting information confidentiality and availability.

·      Identification and Authentication. Helps emphasize that only authorized personnel are allow access to classified or private information or data. Without identification and authentication controls, anyone would have access to sensitive information. The information can be modified or stolen. The implementation of IAUs protects disclosure of information and modification of data, thus promoting information confidentiality and integrity.

·      Physical and Environmental protection controls, makes sure that only individuals with the required clearance may gain access to facilities that contain information systems. These controls also put protocols and procedures in place to prevent the possibility or likelihood of information loss in the event of a disaster occurring (i.e. fire, flood etc.). These controls protect the availability of data thus promoting information availability.

·      System and Information Integrity Controls, when implemented help monitor and control the activities that occur in the system. They inforce the use of tools (IDSs, port scanners etc.) to monitor activities of the system, thus preventing medication of data and promoting information integrity.

·      System and Communication Protection Controls, help protect the shared network channels that information travel through. Protecting the network is crucial because attacker are able to launch attacks that interrupt or intercept data or information moving along shared network channels. These controls enforce the use of tools to encrypt the network channels thus protecting the availability of data and promoting information availability.

INTRODUCTION

The department of veteran’s affairs on this (specific) day experienced a physical security incident. An employee stole a computer system that contained records of millions of veterans. The employee was able to gain access to the room when they systems where keep, and picked one which contained such valuable information. This incident possess a sever threat to the department’s confidentiality and availability of information.

Our organization was been hired to conduct a risk assessment to determine the repercussion of the incident that had occurred. The team consists of twelve members. Members include employees from the business and security unit, and other employees with knowledge of legal, safety, personal, and operation matters. In addition, member from our organization.

Multiple interviews were conducted. We interviewed both senior employees and junior employees of the department, and gathered adequate information about the employee in question and the information that had been stolen.

 

THREAT

We identified the major threat to be the employee. He breached the company policy and made way with a physical computer containing sensitive information about millions of veterans. The disclosure of this information may cause a great deal of problem. It could jeopardize the safety of millions of veterans around the world who have served their country wholeheartedly.

We also identified the use of the sensitive information to blackmail the department. The possibility of the employee or any other individual with access to such sensitive information, threaten to release the information to the public or terrorist groups in exchange for wealth or any other personal gain to be very high. The theft of this sensitive information possess a threat to the department’s confidentiality and availability of information.

 

VULNERABILITY

The major vulnerability that we identified was that there were zero to little physical security protocols put in place to mitigate the risk of such an incident occurring in the first place. This is how the employee was able to make away with the computer system without being apprehended when access the room containing the system or leave the building.

 

After conducting interviews with knowledgeable individuals, the information gathered was then analyzed over a period in order to determine the risk impact. Multiple scenarios were developed which helped with identifying how the threat occurred and the vulnerabilities in the department’s system that were exploited. We adopted and modified categories developed by the department of Defense to categorize the damage and/or loss that the organization may have sustained i.e. the risk that the department could be facing.

 

Category 1:  loss or theft of information, loss or theft of critical or sensitive information (i.e. hardware containing highly classified or sensitive information), disruption of systems, natural disasters

 

Category 2:  access to and modification of information, physical breach of server room or any facility harbouring sensitive information, human error

 

Category 3:  system authentication error, hardware failure, illness, loss of public trust

 

Category 4:  employee error, minor system error

 

The above cites the level of severity of the threats that could potentially occur, category 1 being the highest risk to category 4 being the lowest risk. We then rank the probability of scenarios materializing with the categories below.

 

Category A:  Frequently – possibility of repeated incidents

 

Category B: Probable – possibility of isolated incidents

 

Category C:  Occasional – Possibility of occurring sometimes

 

Category D:  Remote – Not likely to happen


Category E:  Improbable – Practically impossible

 

 

RISK ASSESSMENT MATRIX

 

 

From the risk assessment that we conducted and using the risk assessment matrix to determine the level of risk the Department is to face rank risk 1 as the highest to risk 4 as the lowest, we concluded that the threat ranked on our risk assessment matrix as a risk 1 scenario, which is undesirable and requires immediate corrective action.

 

 

RECOMMENDATIONS

 

After Identifying the threats, vulnerabilities and performing a risk analysis on the current situation. The team has developed a set of recommendations that we believe will help mitigate the risks and vulnerabilities Identified above. These recommendations were derived from the National institute of standards and technology (NIST) recommended security controls, and was adapted to the current situation. They are as follows:

 

PHYSICAL AND ENVIROMETAL PROTECTION

 

PE-1 Physical and environmental protection policy and procedures: the department will need to come up with brand new document with new policies and procedures to mitigate the risk of environmental breaches and what to do if a breach has occurred. This policy will help the organization by standardizing their approach to physical breaches within the department.

 

PE-2 Physical Access Authorization: The department should develop and keep current records of personnel with authorized access to facilities containing information systems. By doing so we will be able to track activities of employees, which building or facilities they enter, where they entered. Thus, giving the department to identify employees accesses where they aren’t meant to immediately.

PE-3 Physical Access Control: The department should implement authentication systems (i.e. biometrics) to control all physical access points (including designated entry/exit points) to facilities containing information systems. In addition, verify individual access authorization before granting access to the facilities. The department should control access to areas officially designated as publicly accessible. Implementing this control will prevent employees from accessing facilities without the appropriate clearance.

 

PE-6 Monitoring Physical access: The department should implement monitoring technologies (like CCTV, radars) to monitor physical access to information systems. Both classified and publicly access areas should be monitored, to detect and respond to incidents on time.
